What is Tā Moko

More than ink.
A second skin
of meaning.

Tā moko is the traditional tattooing practice of the Māori people of New Zealand (Aotearoa). Unlike decorative tattoos, each moko is a deeply personal record — a visible map of a person's whakapapa (genealogy), tribal affiliations, social status, and achievements.

The word tā means "to strike" or "to carve," and moko refers to the skin markings themselves. For Māori, the face was the most sacred part of the body, making tā moko on the face — particularly for men — the ultimate expression of identity.

Women traditionally received moko kauae — tattoos on the chin and lips — signifying lineage, femininity, and strength.

Before 1200 AD

Origins in Eastern Polynesia

Tā moko traces its roots to the ancestral Polynesian homeland of Hawaiki. Early Māori settlers arriving in Aotearoa brought tattooing practices from Eastern Polynesia, where body marking was a sacred rite connecting mortals to the divine. Oral tradition credits the art to Mataora, a young chief who descended to the underworld and learned the sacred craft from Uetonga, lord of the dead.

1200s – 1700s

The Classical Era

During this period, tā moko became the defining mark of Māori identity. Unlike Polynesian cousins who used puncture techniques, Māori masters (tohunga tā moko) used albatross bone chisels — uhitaonga — to carve grooves into the skin, creating deep, ridged designs rather than flat marks. This sculptural quality made Māori moko unique in the Pacific world. The face moko of a high-ranking chief (rangatira) was so individual it was legally equivalent to a signature.

1769

First European Contact

When Captain James Cook arrived in Aotearoa, his naturalist Joseph Banks documented tā moko in extraordinary detail. Banks wrote of the "most perfect symmetry" in the spiral designs, noting that no two were alike. Europeans were simultaneously fascinated and unsettled — they struggled to understand that these marks were not decoration but autobiography. Early colonial artists produced portraits of tattooed chiefs, inadvertently preserving important records of the art form.

1800s

Mokomokai & the Colonial Dark Period

The colonial era brought catastrophic disruption. Demand from European collectors for mokomokai — preserved tattooed heads — created a grotesque trade that led to inter-tribal violence. More damaging still, missionary pressure and colonial laws suppressed Māori cultural practices. By the late 1800s, tā moko had nearly disappeared entirely, regarded by colonisers as a mark of savagery rather than the profound cultural institution it was.

1970s – 1990s

The Māori Renaissance

The Māori cultural renaissance (Māori: te orokohanga) ignited a profound revival of language, arts, and identity. Pioneering artists like Robyn Kahukiwa and Para Matchitt reintegrated traditional motifs into contemporary art. By the 1990s, a new generation of practitioners — led by tohunga like Gordon Toi Hutchings and Te Rangitu Netana — began reclaiming tā moko not as historical artefact, but as living, evolving practice.

Today

Tā Moko Reborn

Tā moko is now experiencing its most significant revival in over 150 years. Māori across Aotearoa and the global diaspora reclaim their moko as an act of sovereignty, healing, and pride. Contemporary tohunga blend traditional chisel techniques with modern tools while maintaining rigorous cultural protocols. Critically, iwi (tribes) and whānau (families) have led important conversations establishing that tā moko is tapu — sacred — and that non-Māori wearing genuine moko constitutes cultural appropriation.

Decoding the designs

Every curve,
every spiral —
a word.

The motifs of tā moko are not random. Each element is a glyph in a visual language, readable by those trained to understand it. Hover each pattern to explore its meaning.

Koru
Unfolding fern frond
The most iconic symbol — an unfurling ponga (silver fern) frond representing new life, growth, strength, and peace. In moko, koru spirals radiate from the nostrils (representing descent from primordial breath) and mark a person's connection to the earth and their ancestors.
Pakati
Dog-skin cloak pattern
Interlocking triangular forms derived from the weaving patterns of kahu kurī — cloaks made from dog skin worn by warriors and chiefs. When incorporated into moko, pakati marks a person's courage in battle and their role as a protector of the iwi (tribe).
Unaunahi
Fish scale motif
Overlapping scale-like shapes representing the ocean's abundance and the significance of fish as a life-sustaining resource. Found most often on people with deep connections to coastal iwi or those whose ancestors were renowned fishermen and navigators of the Pacific.
Ahu ahu mataroa
Athletic achievement
Horizontal bands of varying widths that flow across the cheeks and jaw, recording athletic achievements, physical endurance, and feats of strength. The width and number of bands convey the magnitude of accomplishment — the thickest bands mark the most celebrated deeds.
Haehae
Parallel lines of rank
Parallel curved lines flowing along the contours of the face from brow to jaw. The number of lines and their placement directly encode social rank within the tribal hierarchy. A high chief might bear many fine, closely spaced lines — each one a generation of noble lineage made visible.
Pitau
Prosperity & growth spiral
Curved, tapering forms resembling tendrils or prows of waka (canoes), representing forward movement, prosperity, and growth in all aspects of life. Pitau frequently marks the brow region — close to the seat of thought — signifying intellectual leadership and the ability to guide a community.

Wairua — Spirituality

Sacred skin,
divine
inheritance.

Tā moko is inseparable from Māori cosmology. The designs do not simply decorate — they are tapu (sacred), carrying the mana (spiritual authority) of the wearer's entire ancestral line. To understand tā moko is to understand how Māori see the relationship between past, present, and future.

  • 01

    Whakapapa — Genealogical Record

    Every element of a moko encodes genealogy. A face is literally a family tree — readable by elders who understand its grammar. The left side traditionally represents the father's lineage; the right, the mother's. No two are ever the same, because no two lives are.

  • 02

    Mana — Spiritual Authority

    Mana is the inherited and acquired spiritual power that determines a person's standing in the world. Tā moko is both a record and an amplifier of mana — wearing it correctly increases one's spiritual potency. An untattooed face was considered a blank page, an identity unwritten.

  • 03

    Tapu — The Sacred Prohibition

    The process of receiving tā moko is governed by strict tapu. The tohunga tā moko (master practitioner) undergoes ritual preparation; the recipient observes food restrictions; the act itself is a threshold moment — the person who sits for moko is not the same one who rises afterward. The skin has been remade.

  • 04

    Atua — Connection to the Gods

    Mythology places the origin of tā moko with the atua (gods) themselves. Uetonga taught Mataora the art in Rarohenga (the underworld), and Mataora carried it back to the living world. This divine origin means every moko is a thread connecting its wearer to the atua — a mark of continuity between the mortal and immortal realms.

The living tradition

Reclaimed,
reborn,
resurgent.

After generations of suppression, tā moko is flourishing. The contemporary revival is not mere nostalgia — it is an assertion of sovereignty, a healing of colonial wounds, and a radical act of cultural continuity.

Cultural Sovereignty

Leading iwi and tohunga have established clear cultural protocols around tā moko, distinguishing it from generic "tribal" tattoo designs. The Māori community asserts that genuine tā moko belongs exclusively to those with Māori whakapapa — it is not a style, it is a birthright. This position has gained increasing legal and cultural recognition in Aotearoa and internationally.

Kirituhi for Non-Māori

For non-Māori who wish to honour Pacific or indigenous art forms, kirituhi (skin writing) offers a path — designs inspired by tā moko aesthetics but without the genealogical specificity of genuine moko. This distinction is crucial: kirituhi is crafted for the individual without claiming ancestral meaning, allowing appreciation without appropriation.

Global Māori Identity

With significant Māori populations in Australia, the United Kingdom, and beyond, tā moko has become a global symbol of identity for the diaspora. For many, receiving their moko thousands of miles from Aotearoa is a powerful reclamation — a way to carry home on their skin, to be immediately recognisable as Māori to other Māori anywhere in the world.
